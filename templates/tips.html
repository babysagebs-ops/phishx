Core Defensive Tips
URLs

Always inspect links before clicking

Hover over any link to reveal the real destination in your browser's status bar. The display text and the actual URL can be completely different.

Example: A link saying "PayPal Login" might point to paypa1-secure.ru/login — note the digit 1 instead of the letter l.
HTTPS

HTTPS alone doesn't mean safe

A padlock icon only means the connection is encrypted, not that the site is legitimate. Phishers routinely get free SSL certificates for their fake sites.

What to do: Check the full domain name, not just the padlock. https://paypal.com is safe. https://paypal-secure-login.xyz is not.
Email

Treat unexpected emails with suspicion

Phishers impersonate banks, delivery companies, and tech platforms. Urgency is a red flag — "your account will be closed in 24 hours" is a classic tactic.

Rule of thumb: Legitimate companies never ask you to confirm passwords, PINs, or card numbers by email.
Accounts

Use two-factor authentication

2FA means a stolen password alone isn't enough to access your accounts. Use an authenticator app rather than SMS where possible — SIM-swap attacks exist.

Recommended: Google Authenticator, Authy, or a hardware key (YubiKey) for high-value accounts like email, banking, and work systems.
Sender

Verify the sender address, not the name

Display names can say anything. "Apple Support" is easy to fake — the actual sending address is what matters. Check it carefully for typos or unusual domains.

Suspicious: support@apple-helpdesk.com or no-reply@apple.com.verify-id.net. The real domain is always what comes right before the last dot.
Attachments

Never open unexpected attachments

Malware is often delivered as Word documents, PDFs, or ZIP files disguised as invoices, delivery notices, or job offers. When in doubt, verify with the sender directly.

High-risk file types: .exe, .docm, .xlsm, .js, .vbs — macros inside Office files are a common entry point.
Red Flags to Watch For
Domain name uses numbers substituted for letters (0 for o, 1 for l)
URL has an unusual number of subdomains (e.g. login.verify.bank.com.evil.net)
Email contains spelling mistakes or awkward grammar
Message creates urgent pressure to act immediately
Request for sensitive info like passwords, OTPs, or card numbers
Logos look stretched, blurry, or slightly off-colour
Link destination doesn't match what the email describes
Sender address uses a free webmail domain for an official communication
